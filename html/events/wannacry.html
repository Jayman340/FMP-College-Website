WannaCry (2017)

Summary

WannaCry was a global ransomware attack that began in May 2017, infecting computers across more than 150 countries. It worked by encrypting files on infected systems and demanding a ransom payment in Bitcoin to restore access (Wikipedia, n.d.). The attack spread rapidly due to its worm-like behaviour, allowing it to move automatically across networks without user interaction. It impacted over 300,000 systems worldwide and caused major disruption to businesses and critical infrastructure, including healthcare services (Wikipedia, n.d.).

The attack had a significant impact on the UK's National Health Service (NHS), where many systems were running outdated or unpatched versions of Windows. The ransomware caused major disruption to hospital operations, with at least 34% of NHS trusts affected and thousands of appointments and operations cancelled (NAO, 2017). In some cases, hospitals were unable to access patient records or critical systems, forcing staff to revert to manual processes such as paper records and telephone communication. This disruption affected patient care, with delays to treatment and patients being diverted to other hospitals (ICO, n.d.)

How It Operated

WannaCry spread by exploiting a vulnerability in the Windows Server Message Block (SMB) protocol, using a leaked exploit known as EternalBlue (Cybernews, 2023). This exploit allowed attackers to send specially crafted network packets to vulnerable systems and execute malicious code remotely without authentication (Microsoft, 2008).

Once inside a system, the ransomware encrypted files using strong cryptographic algorithms, making them inaccessible to users. A ransom demand was then displayed, requesting payment in Bitcoin in exchange for a decryption key (CSO Online, 2017). Unlike traditional ransomware, WannaCry functioned as a worm, meaning it could scan networks for other vulnerable systems and infect them automatically. This allowed it to spread extremely quickly across organisations and the internet (SecurityAffairs, 2026). The attack particularly affected outdated or unpatched systems, such as those running older versions of Windows like Windows XP, which remained vulnerable to the exploit (Wikipedia, n.d.).

How It Was Resolved

The rapid spread of WannaCry was slowed when a security researcher, Marcus Hutchins, discovered a “kill switch” within the malware's code. By registering a specific domain name used by the worm, Hutchins was able to prevent further infections and significantly reduce its spread (McAfee, 2017). Microsoft had already released a security patch (MS17-010) before the attack, which fixed the vulnerability exploited by WannaCry. Systems that had applied this update were protected from infection (NHS Digital, 2017).

Following the attack, organisations implemented emergency security measures, including patching vulnerable systems, isolating infected networks, and improving monitoring to prevent further spread. Despite these efforts, the attack highlighted how difficult it can be to fully stop malware once it spreads rapidly across global networks.

What Changed

  • Demonstrated how ransomware could combine with worm capabilities to spread automatically across networks (SecurityAffairs, 2026).
  • Highlighted the risks of unpatched systems, as many organisations had not installed available security updates before the attack (NHS Digital, 2017).
  • Showed how leaked government-developed exploits could be used in large-scale cyberattacks (Cybernews, 2023).
  • Exposed weaknesses in critical infrastructure, with major disruption to healthcare services such as the NHS (NAO, 2017).
  • Increased awareness of ransomware as a major global cybersecurity threat rather than just individual attacks (IBM, n.d.).

Defender Takeaways

  • Systems should be regularly updated with security patches to prevent exploitation of known vulnerabilities (NHS Digital, 2017).
  • Organisations should avoid using outdated software that no longer receives security updates (Wikipedia, n.d.).
  • Network segmentation should be used to limit how far malware can spread within an organisation (SecurityAffairs, 2026).
  • Regular backups should be maintained so data can be recovered without paying ransom demands (CSO Online, 2017).
  • Monitoring and incident response plans should be in place to quickly detect and contain outbreaks (IBM, n.d.).
