NotPetya (2017)

Summary

NotPetya was a major cyberattack that occurred in June 2017 and initially appeared to be ransomware. However, it was later identified as destructive malware designed to permanently damage systems rather than allow recovery after payment (Wikipedia, n.d.).

The attack began in Ukraine but rapidly spread across global networks, affecting major organisations in sectors such as shipping, logistics and energy. It caused widespread disruption and billions of pounds in damages, making it one of the most costly cyberattacks in history (Radware, n.d.).

How It Operated

NotPetya initially spread through a compromised update to a widely used Ukrainian accounting software, allowing attackers to infiltrate networks through a trusted supply chain. Once inside a system, it exploited a Windows vulnerability similar to the one used in WannaCry, allowing it to spread automatically between systems without user interaction.

The malware also used stolen credentials and administrative tools to move laterally across infected networks, making it highly effective in corporate environments (Radware, n.d.). Unlike genuine ransomware, NotPetya overwrote critical system components such as the master boot record (MBR), rendering systems unusable and preventing data recovery.

Although it displayed a ransom demand, the payment system was ineffective, confirming that the attack was designed to cause disruption rather than generate profit (Wikipedia, n.d.).

How It Was Resolved

The spread of NotPetya was slowed through patching vulnerabilities, isolating infected systems and shutting down affected networks to prevent further infection.

Cybersecurity researchers quickly identified that the malware was destructive rather than recoverable ransomware, allowing organisations to focus on containment rather than paying ransom demands.

Due to the irreversible damage caused, many organisations had to rebuild systems from backups or replace infrastructure entirely, highlighting the severity of the attack (Radware, n.d.).

What Changed

  • Demonstrated that malware can be disguised as ransomware while actually being designed purely for destruction (Wikipedia, n.d.).
  • Highlighted the risks of supply chain attacks, where trusted software updates can be used to distribute malware.
  • Showed how attackers can combine multiple techniques such as exploits and credential theft to maximise spread (Radware, n.d.).
  • Reinforced the role of cyberattacks in geopolitical conflict, with NotPetya widely considered an example of state-sponsored cyber warfare.
  • Increased awareness of the need for stronger internal network security and segmentation to limit large-scale damage.

Defender Takeaways

  • Organisations should secure software supply chains to prevent malicious updates being distributed.
  • Systems must be regularly updated to protect against known vulnerabilities (Radware, n.d.).
  • Network segmentation should be implemented to prevent malware spreading across entire organisations.
  • Strong credential management should be enforced to prevent attackers moving laterally across systems.
  • Regular backups should be maintained, as destructive malware may permanently erase data.
