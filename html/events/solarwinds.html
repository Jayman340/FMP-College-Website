SolarWinds (2020)

Summary

The SolarWinds attack was a major cybersecurity breach discovered in December 2020, involving a sophisticated supply chain attack on the SolarWinds Orion software platform. Attackers inserted malicious code into legitimate software updates, allowing them to gain access to thousands of organisations worldwide (CIS, n.d.).

The attack affected approximately 18,000 customers, including government agencies and large companies, making it one of the most significant cyber espionage campaigns in history (Fortinet, 2026).

How It Operated

The attackers infiltrated SolarWinds’ development environment and inserted a backdoor, known as SUNBURST, into the Orion software updates (Microsoft, 2017).

These malicious updates were digitally signed and distributed through official channels, meaning organisations installed them without suspicion as part of routine updates (Fortinet, 2026).

Once installed, the malware remained inactive for a period to avoid detection before communicating with external command-and-control servers to receive instructions (Infosec Institute, 2021).

Attackers then used this access to move laterally across networks, steal sensitive data, and monitor systems over long periods without detection (CISA, n.d.).

The attack was highly selective, with attackers focusing on specific high-value targets after gaining initial access through the supply chain compromise.

How It Was Resolved

The SolarWinds attack was discovered by cybersecurity company FireEye in December 2020 after detecting unusual activity within its own systems (CSO Online, 2020).

Following the discovery, affected organisations were advised to immediately disconnect compromised SolarWinds systems, apply security patches, and investigate potential breaches (CISA, 2021).

Remediation was complex due to the deep level of access attackers had gained, often requiring organisations to rebuild systems, reset credentials, and conduct extensive forensic analysis (CISA, 2021).

Governments and cybersecurity agencies coordinated responses to share threat intelligence and improve detection of similar supply chain attacks in the future.

What Changed

  • Highlighted the dangers of supply chain attacks, where trusted software updates can be used to spread malware (Fortinet, 2026).
  • Demonstrated that attackers can remain undetected for long periods by blending in with legitimate network activity (Infosec Institute, 2021).
  • Showed the importance of securing software development processes and build environments (CIS, n.d.).
  • Reinforced the role of nation-state actors in cyber espionage and large-scale attacks (CISA, n.d.).
  • Led to increased focus on supply chain security and zero trust security models in organisations worldwide (CISOMEET, 2025).

Defender Takeaways

  • Organisations should secure their software supply chains and verify the integrity of updates before deployment.
  • Continuous monitoring is essential to detect unusual behaviour rather than relying solely on perimeter security (CISOMEET, 2025).
  • Zero trust security models should be implemented to reduce reliance on trusted internal systems.
  • Access controls and credential management should be strengthened to prevent attackers moving freely within networks (CISA, n.d.).
  • Incident response plans must include third-party suppliers, as attacks may originate outside the organisation.
