Data Extortion

Summary

Data extortion is a modern cybercrime trend where attackers steal sensitive data and threaten to release it unless a ransom is paid. Unlike traditional ransomware, attackers do not always encrypt files but instead rely on the threat of exposure to pressure organisations (IBM, n.d.).

This method has become increasingly common since around 2019, as attackers realised that organisations are more likely to pay to avoid reputational damage, legal consequences and data breaches.

How It Operates

Attackers typically gain access to systems through phishing, vulnerable services, or stolen credentials. Once inside, they move through the network to locate and extract valuable data, such as customer records, financial information, or internal documents.

The data is then copied and transferred to external servers controlled by the attackers. After exfiltration, the attackers demand payment, threatening to publish or sell the stolen data if the ransom is not paid (CrowdStrike, n.d.).

Some attacks also combine encryption and extortion, known as “double extortion”, where attackers both lock systems and steal data to increase pressure on victims.

How It Is Resolved

Responding to data extortion attacks involves identifying and removing the attackers from the network, securing compromised systems, and investigating the extent of data loss.

Organisations may involve law enforcement and cybersecurity specialists to manage the incident and reduce further damage.

Unlike ransomware, recovery is difficult because stolen data cannot be retrieved once exfiltrated. As a result, organisations must focus on containment, legal response, and notifying affected individuals.

What Changed

  • Shifted cybercrime from system disruption to data theft and reputational damage.
  • Reduced reliance on encryption, making attacks effective even if systems are backed up.
  • Increased pressure on organisations through legal and regulatory risks (e.g. GDPR fines).
  • Demonstrated how attackers adapt techniques to bypass improved ransomware defences.
  • Highlighted the importance of protecting sensitive data, not just systems.

Defender Takeaways

  • Organisations should monitor for unusual data transfers to detect potential data exfiltration.
  • Strong access controls and least privilege should be enforced to limit attacker movement.
  • Sensitive data should be encrypted and properly secured to reduce impact if stolen.
  • Incident response plans should include procedures for handling data breaches and extortion attempts.
  • Regular security awareness training can help prevent phishing attacks and credential theft.
