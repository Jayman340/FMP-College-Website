Mydoom (2004)

Summary

Mydoom was a mass-mailing computer worm first identified in January 2004 and is widely considered the fastest-spreading email worm in history. It spread mainly through malicious email attachments, using social engineering techniques to trick users into opening infected files (Huntress, 2025).

Within a very short period, Mydoom infected hundreds of thousands of computers worldwide and caused major disruption to internet services. At its peak, it generated a significant proportion of global email traffic and impacted organisations across multiple sectors (Wikipedia, n.d.).

How It Operated

Mydoom spread primarily through email messages designed to look like delivery failures or system-generated notifications. These emails contained malicious attachments, which executed the worm when opened by the user (ExpressVPN, 2026).

Once activated, Mydoom harvested email addresses from infected systems, including address books and local files, and used this data to send copies of itself to new targets. It used its own internal SMTP engine, allowing it to send large volumes of email without relying on the user’s email client (Radware, n.d.).

In addition to email-based propagation, Mydoom also copied itself into peer-to-peer file sharing networks such as Kazaa, increasing its spread across shared systems (Wikipedia, n.d.).

A key technical feature of Mydoom was its ability to install a backdoor on infected machines. This allowed attackers to remotely control compromised systems and use them as part of a botnet to carry out further malicious activity (Huntress, 2025).

The worm also included a payload capable of launching distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks against specific targets, including major organisations such as SCO Group and Microsoft (ExpressVPN, 2026).

How It Was Resolved

The response to Mydoom involved rapid action from cybersecurity organisations and antivirus companies. Detection signatures and removal tools were quickly developed and distributed, allowing infected systems to be identified and cleaned (Huntress, 2025).

Organisations implemented stricter email filtering and spam detection systems to reduce the worm’s ability to spread through email communication (Radware, n.d.).

In addition, network monitoring was improved to detect unusual behaviour, such as high volumes of outgoing email traffic or participation in DDoS attacks. These measures helped limit the impact of the worm and prevent further spread.

In the long term, Mydoom contributed to major improvements in email security, including better attachment filtering, improved spam detection, and stronger endpoint protection systems (ExpressVPN, 2026).

What Changed

  • Demonstrated that malware could spread at an unprecedented speed, becoming the fastest-spreading email worm in history at the time (Wikipedia, n.d.).
  • Showed how combining social engineering with automated mass-mailing could create extremely large-scale infections (Radware, n.d.).
  • Introduced widespread use of infected machines as botnets, allowing attackers to control systems remotely and launch coordinated attacks (Huntress, 2025).
  • Highlighted the growing threat of distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks as part of malware campaigns (ExpressVPN, 2026).
  • Marked a shift towards financially motivated cybercrime, with malware being used for profit rather than experimentation (Wikipedia, n.d.).

Defender Takeaways

  • Users should avoid opening unexpected email attachments, especially messages that appear to be system errors or delivery failures (ExpressVPN, 2026).
  • Email filtering and spam detection systems should be used to block malicious attachments before they reach users (Radware, n.d.).
  • Systems should be monitored for unusual behaviour, such as large volumes of outgoing email traffic, which may indicate infection (Huntress, 2025).
  • Organisations should restrict the ability for programs to send emails automatically without authorisation (Radware, n.d.).
  • Network security measures should be used to detect and block DDoS activity originating from infected devices (Wikipedia, n.d.).
